Over the past week, we've been looking over your data to determine what needs to be changed or added before converting to OpenTrails.
What makes trail data OpenTrails-ready?
As you know from reading over the OpenTrails Specification, there are certain fields that are required as part of the spec.
For each agency's data, we are looking to answer a few questions:
Is there both a trailheads dataset and a trail segments dataset? How complete are these datasets?
Are these datasets in shapefile format?
Does each trail segment or trailhead have a unique ID field?
Is the naming convention for that unique ID field consistent among the datasets?
Do the files have the required usage attributes as defined in the specification? Are those attributes correctly formatted?
Is each trail segment and trailhead associated with a single steward?
Based on a cursory assessment of the submitted data, most of you are almost ready to convert!
But, quickly, we are going to go over some common hurdles we need to clear before we can all convert our data.

FIRST: Collecting and maintaining accurate trailhead data.
Trailheads are included in the OpenTrails Specification because they serve as points of entry to the trail network.
They also show amenities available at those locations, which is helpful information for trailgoers!
But how can we collect this data? There are a few different ways.

And traditional methods, like adding the trailheads manually to your GIS or collecting points with a GPS unit.
Again, trailhead data is very important for trailgoers.
SECOND: Determing a system for giving each feature a unique ID that perpetuates across datasets.
Although there is no canonical system for this, the OpenTrails Specification has a suggested method:
[the 'url' field from the corresponding steward in stewards.csv] + [feature type] + [name of individual feature]
Examples include:

  • "http://countyparkdistrict.gov/stewards/0"
  • "http://countyparkdistrict.gov/trail_segments/31-pinetrail"
  • "http://countyparkdistrict.gov/named_trails/5"
  • "http://countyparkdistrict.gov/trailheads/wicker_trailhead"
  • "http://countyparkdistrict.gov/areas/sandrun"
To reiterate a point from last week, you do not need to manage your data internally using the OpenTrails Specification.
Any changes you need to make to the data before conversion can be done separately from your existing data workflow.

Just to familiarize you, we will walk through the conversion process you will be undertaking this week.
As far as preparing your data for the OpenTrails Converter, you have two options.
(Both options are outlined in the OpenTrails Guidebook, and we will suggest to you which is best based on your data.)
With both options, what we're doing is massaging our existing datasets just enough that the converter knows how to make OpenTrails GeoJSON an CSV files from it.
As a quick reminder, these are the five files in an OpenTrails dataset:
stewards.csv: organizations that serve as the primary point of contact for inquiries about trails and trailheads
trail_segments.geojson: physical segments of trails; typically multiple segments will make up a named trail (line features)
named_trails.csv: named trails as defined by park agencies
trailheads.geojson: points of access for trailgoers (point features)
areas.geojson parks and other protected lands (polygon features)
PREPARATION METHOD #1: Convert to Shapefiles and update field names.
The OpenTrails specification consists of five files, but the converter generates those five outputs from three inputs.
Note: the converter does not yet convert area files.
In order for the converter to work its magic, the input files need to have a schema that the converter recognizes.
For each dataset, there are some required fields and some optional fields. We will focus on the required fields.
(However, the more of the optional fields you can provide, the more effective your OpenTrails data will be in serving your trailgoers.)
The first dataset you need to prepare is your trail segments shapefile.
From this file, the converter will create both trail_segments.geojson and named_trails.csv.
For this file, the two required fields are a unique trail ID and a trail name.
After converting those fieldnames, your next step is to add as many of the recommended fields as possible.
If your data has any additional attributes, the converter will ignore and remove them.
The second dataset you need to prepare is your trailheads shapefile.
Similar to trail segments, trailheads has two required fields: a unique trailhead ID and a trailhead name.
Note: if you don't assign your trailheads a unique ID, the converter will assign one for you, but we recomment that you come up with a scheme for doing this internally.
Once you have these two shapefiles prepared, you can create a relationship between the two datasets. This is not required, but is encouraged as it enables functionality with your data.
I won't go into this step-by-step, but ultimately you are taking the unique IDs of the trail segments and adding them to a new field in your trailheads file.
The detailed, step-by-step instructions on how to do this are in the OpenTrails Guidebook.
Once you've made this connection, you will add the recommended fields to the trailheads shapefile.
Boom! Now you're ready for the converter!
