The OpenTrails Specification!
Again, the goal of the specification is to maintain and provide data in service to visitors and trailgoers. The spec was made with this in mind.
The spec contains five different datasets:
stewards.csv: organizations that serve as the primary point of contact for inquiries about trails and trailheads
trail_segments.geojson: physical segments of trails; typically multiple segments will make up a named trail (line features)
named_trails.csv: named trails as defined by park agencies
trailheads.geojson: points of access for trailgoers (point features)
areas.geojson parks and other protected lands (polygon features)
Notice that these are CSV and GeoJSON files.
What is GeoJSON?
GeoJSON is a geographic data format.
It is an extension of JSON, which stands for JavaScript Object Notation.
This means that websites and web applications can read it easily.
GeoJSON has its own specification for what must be included.
Fortunately, there are many awesome tools for getting our spatial data into GeoJSON.
CSV stands for comma-separated values.
Similar to GeoJSON, we can export our data to CSV fairly easily.
GeoJSON and CSV are very popular in the web and open data space.
OpenTrails uses these formats to reach as many users as possible by providing data that is easily interoperable.
The spec includes five files, but they're all connected to each other and rely on each other.
Trail segments are pieces of trail that, when put together, make up complete named trails.
A trail segment contains the IDs of named trails of which it is a part (as there may be multiple) as well as the steward ID of its primary steward.
It also contains information on the various physical characteristics of that section of trail.
There may be multiple agencies that are responsible for parts of trail, but the specification asks for the primary steward.
Named trails is a non-spatial file that contains a list of trails.
A named trail contains the trail segments that, when put together, make up the trail, as well as a list of any other larger trails of which it may be a part.
It also contains the steward ID of its primary steward.
Trailheads mark the points of access for trails.
A trailhead contains:
  • the IDs of trail segments to which it has access
  • a list of named trails to which it has access
  • the area in which it's located
  • the steward ID of its primary steward
Stewards contains a list of names and contact information for any trail stewarding agency in your area.
EVERYTHING relates back to a steward.
Areas (optional) refer to parks or protected lands.

Some quick notes on the specification:
FIRST: The built-in options for attributes are limited.
OpenTrails contains the attributes that are most relevant for park visitors, as determined by lengthy research.
And remember how I said that OpenTrails and OSM were BFFs?
The attributes in the data relate directly to attributes in OSM!
It's pretty neat.
That said, please let us know about attributes you'd like to see.
SECOND: OpenTrails is not meant to replace your existing trail data management (although, feel free).
This specification is meant to aid in publishing useful data, so it can exist entirely outside your existing workflow.
But this is totally up to you.
THIRD: You will maintain control of your authoritative data.
You are the one who gets to say what is legitimate data.
You are the deciders!
FOURTH: Publishing data this way means you get to make and use really cool stuff.
We'll talk more about that in the coming weeks!
In the meantime, be sure to check out opentraildata.org to learn more about the awesome stuff you can do with OpenTrails.
